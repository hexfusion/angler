Fishing and the Environment
Cannonsville Reservoir Water Levels for 2003 where a far cry from 2002, as our winter was cold and wet, and the summer even wetter.  The West, East, and Main Delaware Rivers fished like a house on fire early in the season, with MAJOR BIG fish taking dry flies like candy.  Our guides got so used to getting 20 inch + fish for their clients that they had real withdrawal when the size was UNDER 20 inches!! 
We all had some excellent fishing opportunities with a myriad of hatches.  The Hendrickson hatch was the largest we have seen in 6 years or so.  Ephemerella subvaria started early and appeared for well over a month, with big fish keying on them continually.  Sizes 14 and 16, males and females, it was to die for!  March Browns were good too, and so were the Early Black Stones.  Paraleps aka blue quills were also a strong hatch.  The drakes began in earnest in late May and early June, with the Green Drake adults and Coffin Flies bringing up the big hungry Browns and Rainbows to dine in style.  In fact, the Drakes lasted for several weeks, much much longer than normal.  As usual caddis were in abundance with various colors and genus working all through the system.  Tan caddis in particular were excellent, with Spent Caddis in tan being most effective.  The only true disappointment was with the Sulphur hatch in June.  Though there were some nice hatches of Invaria, Dorethea, and even a few Rotunda on the main stem, over all the hatch was anemic to say the least.
The spinner falls this past season were feast or famine.  There were times when it would happen when it shouldn't have, and other times we expected it and it didn't happen.  Ahhh...that's why the call it fishing!
The mid season slow down of hatches occurred per usual, but not for long as the trico hatch was fairly good, and the minute, size 20 and 22 flying ants also appeared in August.  We had hebe's in September, and the Isonychia were good in August and September too.  In late September and October there were good pseudo action with sizes ranging from 22 to 28, and a few Autumn Sedge in size 12 as well.  All in all it was a great season.  We had healthy fish, and a good spawn.  We caught big fish in the early season, then smaller Fish in mid-season, and back to larger fish at the end of the season.

 The West Branch of the Delaware River for 2004 has all of us musing as to just what to expect.  Throwing caution to the wind, we will toss out an educated guess.  If patterns hold true, we have swung to a feast of water in 2003, from the famine of H²O in 2002.  So it stands to reason that the pendulum will swing back, but hopefully not as far.  The theory sounds good!....but will it actually work out that way?  Farmer's Almanacs, Crystal Balls, and Ouji Boards are all being ground together with the mortar and pestle of visionary insight so we can come up with the definitive answer.  MMMmmm....isn't working.
Let me say here and now, that with magic put aside, this great river system seems to somehow always offer good fishing opportunities somewhere along the stretches of one of the Delaware River tributaries no matter which hydrological condition exists.  In addition realistic goals are being worked for by the Delaware River Foundation, other conservation groups, as well as political action committees, to protect and insure the safety of the river system, which of course includes the trout and invertebrates that abound here.  These organizations are working primarily as volunteer/non-profit organizations, with contributions as their life-blood.  So far with generosity from those of us who love the river system they are making good headway.  Your contribution to one of these groups, either of time or money, can be a great assistance to making this river system become better every year.
So, will the fishing be good in 2004?  YOU BET!  It will be good because the fish will be here. The Mayflies and Caddis will be here.  The opportunity to put a dry fly over a rising fish will be here for those wishing to try.  Will it be easy?  Never.  But if it were easy, then everyone would do it.

 WBA Guide Fishing/Shooting Tips and Fly Tying

The Forgotten Wet Fly. 

by John Dembeck

Many modern fly fishermen, especially those who have started in the last ten years, are blissfully unaware of the oldest, easiest, and often the most effective fly-fishing technique of all. Nymphs are “in” and wet flies are “passé”, but anglers are making a mistake if they don’t get reacquainted with the classic soft hackles and winged wets. 
Wets are the most versatile of all fly types. You can dead drift them, swing them, or do both on one cast. You can fish them deep or at the surface. A damp (barely sunk) wet fly was the original emerger. 
The original Yorkshire soft hackle fishermen worked quartering upstream casts with a dead drift back to them; sort of nymphing with no weight. Today most people think of quartering down stream or across stream casts with a rather fast swing. I often do a combination of both. Cast across or across slightly upstream and immediately do a vigorous back mend to keep the current from pulling the line and fly downstream. Watch the end of your fly line during this initial dead drift phase across from your position. If the line does anything weird- set up. Since your backmend removed/ delayed tension on the line, the fly will sink and slowly drift. Use smaller corrective mends to delay the current from causing a downstream belly, which speeds up the fly. What you’re trying to do is get a smooth transition from a sinking dead drift to a slow rising swing. Lead the line downstream with your rod tip till the line straightens below you and let it hang for a few seconds. 
Most strikes come during the initial dead drift, at the beginning of the swing, and at the end of the swing below you. Initially you must see the strike by watching the line, but once the line pulls tight and swings, you will feel everything. Sometimes the fish will hook themselves, and sometimes you will miss a lot of light strikes. It depends on how aggressive or timid the fish are that day. 
This is probably the best method for searching a lot of water and is an excellent way to explore new streams. It tends to work best in medium to shallow riffles, pocket water, and the heads and tails of pools.  I always fish with two or three wets spaced about 24 inches apart. With this rig fished as described above, you can present different flies with different action to every fish in a riffle and let them make the choice. It’s a good way to learn what’s going on quickly and then adjust. 
The best wet fly that I’ve used over the last 40 years would be the Leadwing Coachman, hands down. Other winged wets that are deadly are the Gold Rib Hare’s Ear and the Light Cahill. My favorite soft hackles are the Partridge & Pheasant Tail, March Brown Spider, Grouse & Green, and Tup’s Indispensable. Sizes from No. 8 to 18 all have their days on fertile rivers like the Delaware. 
Rig some up, get in touch with history, and catch some fish. Good Luck.

 Success Shooting Sporting Clays

by Joe Demalderis 

Early in the 1900’s, the British developed a clay target shooting game designed to prepare the shooter for the hunt.  In the 1980’s this clay target game crossed the Atlantic and now in the 21st Century it has found its way to West Branch Angler.  Some call sporting clays “golf with a shotgun” as the game is played around a course of several different stations offering different target presentations at each. 
Our course features twelve professionally designed stations with two automatic traps (target throwers) at each.  This allows us to throw targets in directions and angles that a course with manual traps could not safely present.  Usually fifty or a hundred targets are thrown. 
Our stations are set in natural surroundings and feature targets that simulate grouse, teal, wood ducks, driven pheasants, running rabbits, decoying ducks, flushing quail, and woodcock, to just name a few.   Our custom designed “off road” clays cars transport you to the course and allow you the option of walking or riding from one station to the next.  On your next visit to our resort bring along your shotgun or borrow one of ours and test your wing shooting skills.  I can’t think of a more fun way to fill the time between the morning and evening rise. 
Never shot before? No problem.  Our trappers are well versed in firearm safety and can provide you with instruction and tips to get you breaking some targets.  We also have a special introduction package available. We require everyone on the course to wear eye and ear protection.  Maximum shotgun loads are 12 gauge 1.1/8 oz of 7.1/2 shot.  Ammo (12 & 20 gauge), shooting glasses, and ear protection are available at the fly shop, or bring your own.  Reservations are required.  If you are coming with a group, let us arrange a fun shoot for you either as part of a fishing package or as a separate activity.  Corporate and social outings are our specialty.

 My First Season Fishing The West Branch

by Ben Sheard
As I drove over the Hale Eddy Bridge I wondered if the decision I made was a wise one.  Moving 500 miles away from my home in Ohio, to a place I had never been, all because I would have the chance to live near and fish a world-class trout river.
Arriving the first week of May the Blue Quills, Hendrickson’s and various caddis were the bugs to expect on the West Branch.  That was the place to go, I was told, after conferring with Larry, the West Branch guides, and whoever else was in the fly shop swapping stories at the time.  The main Delaware was too high to wade and the West Branch was dropping to around 1000 cubic feet per second, which is not that easily waded.   After settling in and purchasing a New York fishing license, I asked Larry at the fly shop for some advice on some local places where I could gain access to the river.  Larry told me of a dozen public access points within 5 miles, so the choice was mine.
I soon found out the West Branch is a big river, much larger than the small spring creeks and mountain streams where I usually fished for trout.  I also quickly found out that this big river has some big, wary browns and rainbows.  As anyone who has fished the West Branch can attest, it doesn’t take long standing amongst a pod of rising 15-20 inch wild trout to realize the fishing can be good.  Real good!  And fishing is what I was doing.  Not catching that many fish, considering all the opportunities I was given.  But the ones I caught were nice, healthy, wild, technical to catch and above all rewarding fish.  I quickly realized I needed to adjust my fishing techniques and equipment to improve my success.  Those adjustments were made easily with help from my experienced and friendly co-workers at the West Branch Angler.  I gathered tips on flies, tying materials, casting, mending line and much, much more. 
There were two tips that helped me the most, being a Delaware newcomer.  First, use fairly sparse, low-riding flies when fishing dries.  Deer hair comparaduns, CDC comparaduns and various emerger styles seem to be good patterns that work well.  Emergers seem to work very well during most hatches, especially during a really “heavy” hatch.  Many times these fish have so many insects to choose from they will “key-in” on a vulnerable stage of the bug and take only that stage of the insect.  This happened a lot to me early in the spring.  There would be duns covering the water, trout sipping all around me, and I would watch in anticipation as my pretty dun imitation drifted drag free right over the feeding fish.  Soon I realized that a good snowshoe rabbit foot or CDC emerger was my “go-to” fly during a hatch.  A good dry fly cripple or wet fly also can be deadly at such times.
The other tip I picked up seemed to be equally important if not more important than the first.  Always attempt to make a quartering downstream cast to the fish and make sure there is no drag.  In some rivers casting upstream to rising fish can be fairly effective but can prove to be very tough on the Delaware.  If you get upstream from a fish, undetected, you now need to make a good cast that puts the line on the water correctly.  A good reach cast is a way to put your line in a position so it doesn’t cause your fly to drag prematurely.  Sometimes you are casting 20 feet, other times 70, but a good reach at the end will definitely help out.  This is where good gear will come in handy.  The slow, 6 ½-8-foot rods that I have for fishing those smaller streams need another foot or two to really be effective on the Delaware.  I can’t tell you how much it helps to have a good, high-performance fly rod that is at least 9 feet when fishing the Delaware.  These longer, faster rods allow you to reach more fish, cast in the wind and reduce the amount of fish-spooking false casts when making any cast.
Looking back on my decision to move to New York and fish the Delaware, was it a good one?  Well, my first week I caught several fish on dries over 19 inches and had a brown swim within feet of me that had to be 30 inches.  Definitely a good decision!

How I learned to stay focused on work and still go fly fishing. 

by Eric Balinski

 “… a key aspect of highly visionary companies: They do not oppress themselves with what we call the Tyranny of the OR – the rational view that cannot easily accept paradox, which cannot live with two seemingly contradictory forces or ideas at the same time.  The Tyranny of OR pushes people to believe that things must be either A OR B but not both.”

I am either a low cost supplier or I’m an innovator
I create wealth for shareholders or I do good in the world
I stay focused on completing my work or I go fly fishing

 “Instead of being oppressed by the Tyranny of the OR, highly visionary companies liberate themselves with the Genius of the AND – the ability to embrace both extremes of a number of dimensions at the same time.  Instead of choosing between A OR B, they figure out a way to have both A AND B.”  For Example:

We create profit and we help the world become a better place
We must be more productive and we create innovation
We stay focused on work and we go fly fishing 
Quotes From:  Built to Last, by Jim Collins and Jerry Porras, 1994         

           The Leadership on the Fly™ workshops now held at West Branch Angler embraces the Genius of AND, helping people accomplish things that they didn’t think were possible within their company or organization.  It unleashes the power of possibility, vision, creativity, yet is grounded in practical business principles that can help any organization, in any situation improve its performance.       
The principles behind Leadership on the Fly began to form in the early 90’s when I took over the corporate Marketing Training for a Fortune 100 firm.  We employed a stable of the finest MBA level professors from such places as Harvard, Wharton, Kellogg, and University of Michigan.   Now, responsible for preparing a training program, it occurred to me:  Do these concepts that we teach actually help improve our company’s results?  Little did I know then that this simple question would forever change my life. The answers I received from our “stable of experts” regarding this questions was always similar: trust the ideas of Marketing.    In one regard, this reply appealed to me having been taught these same lessons as a Marketing major in college.  Instead, I decided to challenge these ideas and to learn for myself what truly drives company performance.  Ironically, my research suggested that it was possible to build a successful, profitable company without a Marketing department, and results could be sustained for long periods of time.
By 1993, I was introduced to Jim Collins, a rising luminary at Stanford University. Our paths crossed at just the right time to help me challenge some long-held views about business and organizations.  Jim was then researching the question: How do some companies become truly “great” companies.  One idea Jim advanced, the Genius of And, didn’t reveal its promise until I started teaching personnel inside my company. 
It quickly became apparent that people had huge mental blocks to learning and embracing the new ideas. However, a difficult consulting project in 1996 helped me overcome this.  The project required teaching 125 international cross-functional people a newly developed company strategy.   Though I had taught many workshops already, this particular company leader made it clear: I had to use relevant examples that would part the clouds and cause complete acceptance of the new strategy, AND do it in only 4 hours.  After numerous false starts, I had to admit that I was stumped. I finally escaped for my annual fall salmon fly fishing trip with a bunch of friends to the Pere Marquette River in Michigan.  Amongst fish and friends, the fast approaching deadline stirred the idea of using fish and fishing as a metaphor to explain business.  Serendipity is sometimes the offspring of desperation.  AND what I found in that meeting and every workshop since is whether a person fishes or not, no matter their homeland, or gender, this metaphor is a powerful way to break through the mental barriers that hold our businesses and people back.  AND while not everyone is a master angler, mastery of the principles can make a person a better leader, businessperson, human being, and perhaps a better angler too. 
Many of you may think that you must choose between going fly fishing or going to work or going to the many important meetings on your schedule.  Leadership on the Fly will teach you that these are not exclusive pursuits.  Now, the next step in your journey is to embrace the Power of AND as I did:

We need to develop our organization and fly fishing will guide our understanding of ourselves and others.
We need to reach our goals and can achieve them while fly fishing with our customers.
We must complete our important strategy meeting and we can use the insight of fly fishing to teach us.
We must innovate and our natural surroundings hold the lessons to stir our minds with creative thought. 

The Power of Leadership on the Fly is that it shows people the purity of the passion that drives us beyond the quantity of fish we catch.  As fly fisher on the east coast, we have experienced phenomenal fishing. Each experience with the outdoors holds lessons that can be carried back into our companies, organizations, and personal lives.  We can catch lots of fish AND we can be responsible to others, the environment, and the resources we are blessed with.  Learn to embrace the Power of AND in your life and business and never see your business the same again.
